Middle Point Landfill Keeps Racking Up Violations — Here's What It Means for Rutherford County Residents

The state has cited Murfreesboro's Middle Point Landfill again. Locals are frustrated. And if you've got junk to haul, you're probably wondering what this all means for you. We have answers.

By Trash Titans Published April 1, 2026 📍 Murfreesboro, TN

If you've noticed a smell coming from the direction of Old Nashville Highway over the past year, you're not imagining things. Murfreesboro's Middle Point Landfill — operated by Republic Services subsidiary BFI Waste Systems — has been at the center of a growing controversy, and as of late March 2026, it just got more complicated.

📰
Source: WSMV4 News Nashville
According to reporting by WSMV4's Li Cohen, the Tennessee Department of Environment and Conservation issued new violations against the Middle Point Landfill on March 9, 2026. The violations include unsatisfactory litter control with windblown waste observed outside containment areas, unsatisfactory initial cover, and concerns related to ongoing monitoring of leachate conditions.

This comes on the heels of the city of Murfreesboro receiving its 4,000th odor complaint about the facility — a milestone reached back in October 2025 that prompted Mayor Shane McFarland to publicly call it one of the worst-run landfills in the country.

What the Violations Actually Mean

State inspections of solid waste facilities like Middle Point check for a range of environmental and operational standards. When the Tennessee Department of Environment and Conservation issues a Notice of Violation, it means the facility fell short of one or more of those standards during inspection. Here's what the latest round covered:

March 9, 2026 — TDEC Violations Issued

Windblown waste beyond containment areas — Litter and debris from the landfill was found scattered outside the designated containment zones. This is both an environmental concern and a quality-of-life issue for nearby neighborhoods.

Unsatisfactory initial cover — Daily cover (typically soil or alternative material) is required to be applied to exposed waste at the end of each operating day. The inspection found this wasn't meeting standards.

Leachate monitoring concerns — Leachate is liquid that drains through decomposing waste and can carry contaminants. While no discharge into stormwater ditches was observed at the time, the monitoring protocols were flagged as inadequate.

For context, these violations arrived just one month after a Davidson County Chancery Court allowed Murfreesboro officials to halt the review of BFI's application to expand the site vertically — a proposal that would have stacked an estimated 19 million additional tons of waste on top of what's already there.

"This is not an isolated incident — this is a pattern."

— Melissa Farley, Rutherford Forward, via WSMV4

The landfill's operator, for its part, maintains that the March inspection "did not identify any serious violations" and that the facility continues to work with regulators to address any flagged items. Community organization Rutherford Forward disagrees and has been pushing back hard against any expansion plans.

Why This Story Matters Beyond the Smell

The Middle Point situation is about more than a bad odor. It touches on real questions about how fast-growing communities like Murfreesboro handle their waste as the population expands. Rutherford County has been one of the fastest-growing counties in the entire country for years. More people means more waste, and the infrastructure to handle it responsibly has to keep pace.

The push by BFI to vertically expand the landfill — adding 70 feet of waste on top of a facility already generating thousands of odor complaints — understandably has residents angry. The court's decision to pause that review process was a significant win for groups like Rutherford Forward who've been fighting the expansion.

But it also raises a longer-term question that the county will have to answer: where does the waste go? That's a conversation that involves better recycling infrastructure, more aggressive donation and reuse programs, and yes — responsible junk removal businesses that think about what happens after the truck leaves your driveway.

What You Can Do Right Now

If the Middle Point situation has you thinking about how to declutter your home, garage, or property responsibly, here are a few practical steps:

Donate first. Furniture, clothing, appliances and household goods in decent condition don't need to go to a landfill at all. Habitat for Humanity ReStore in Murfreesboro accepts a wide range of items. Goodwill, local Facebook groups, and Buy Nothing groups are all options.

Separate your metals. Scrap metal — old grills, appliances, bed frames, pipes — can be recycled and stays completely out of the waste stream. Any reputable junk removal company should be pulling metals separately.

Ask your junk removal company where stuff goes. It's a fair question. If they can't answer it, that tells you something. At Trash Titans, we're happy to walk you through our disposal process before we ever load the first item.

Stay informed on the landfill fight. Rutherford Forward and local news outlets like WSMV4 have been covering this story closely. The more residents stay engaged, the more pressure there is on regulators and facility operators to do the right thing.
